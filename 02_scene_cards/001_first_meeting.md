# First Meeting

## Scene Function

Introduce the formative New Jersey flashback: Nathan's isolation as an older Korean undergraduate, the morally awkward social world around the Korean graduate students, and the first moment he notices Elaine.

The scene should explain why Elaine's arrival matters without turning Nathan's past into self-justification.

## POV Character

Nathan.

## Time and Place

Past timeline. A fictional New Jersey state university, late 1990s or early 2000s.

Primary location: Nathan's shared house near campus.

## External Event

Nathan, newly studying computer science in America after abandoning his earlier path in Korea, lives in a shared house and struggles to fit into campus life. An older Korean doctoral student across the hall asks him to help prepare dinner for several guests. Nathan expects graduate-school acquaintances.

Four young women arrive instead. Nathan understands too late that the older student has arranged the dinner with flirtation in mind. In the doorway or early moments of the dinner, Nathan sees Elaine for the first time.

## Internal Shift

Before Elaine arrives, Nathan's loneliness feels like a private defect: his age, his English, his changed academic path, his inability to belong.

After he sees Elaine, loneliness becomes desire. Not only sexual desire, but the desire to be seen by someone who seems to move through America with a freedom he does not yet possess.

## Conflict

Nathan wants to belong without admitting how badly he wants it.

What blocks him:

- His age and background make him feel out of sequence.
- His English limits his confidence.
- The older Korean student's motives make the evening feel compromised.
- Elaine's confidence makes Nathan aware of everything unfinished in himself.

## Subtext

Nathan is afraid he has mistaken escape for reinvention.

The older student is afraid of aging into invisibility and masks it with appetite.

Elaine, if glimpsed only from Nathan's POV, should not be reduced to beauty. Her presence should suggest another life: American ease, immigrant adaptation, and a guardedness Nathan cannot yet read.

## Key Image

Nathan standing in the shared-house kitchen with the smell of oil, beer, and overcooked sausage in the air, hearing women's voices at the door before he sees Elaine.

## Ending Hook

Nathan sees Elaine and recognizes, before knowing anything about her, that the evening has become dangerous in a way he cannot explain.

## Privacy Check

- Use fictional names only.
- Keep the university fictional.
- Remove exact institutional titles and real departments when they could identify people.
- Make the older Korean student a composite.
- Do not reuse exact dialogue from the source.
- Do not identify the landlord's employer.

## Fictionalized Scene Material

Use this as tonal direction, not final chapter text:

Nathan had learned the house by its noises before he learned it by affection: the pipes knocking awake before dawn, the landlord's shoes on the stairs, the microwave door closing in the kitchen with the soft violence of shared living. In Korea, he had been late but legible. In New Jersey, he was simply late.

The younger students on campus carried their futures carelessly, as if a major were a jacket they could change when the weather turned. Nathan carried his like a borrowed coat. Computer science sounded clean when he explained it to people back home. In English, under fluorescent classroom light, it became a list of things he did not know.

That evening he was cutting onions because Victor had asked him to help.

"Just a few people," Victor had said.

Nathan had assumed he meant the Korean graduate students: men with tired eyes, women with practical haircuts, someone always arguing about politics over beer that had been bought too early and drunk too warm. He did not expect the voices at the door to be young, bright, female.

Then Elaine stepped in behind the others, and the hallway seemed to narrow around her.

She was not beautiful in the delicate way Korean men praised when they wanted agreement. She was tall, sun-browned, composed in her own skin. She removed her shoes without making a performance of knowing how. She looked once toward the kitchen, not long enough to invite anything, but long enough for Nathan to become aware of the knife in his hand, the onion on the board, the shirt he had not thought to change.

Victor was already smiling too broadly.

Nathan looked down before anyone could catch him looking. It was the first cowardice Elaine would never know about.
