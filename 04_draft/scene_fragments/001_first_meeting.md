## Draft Fragment

Nathan had learned the house by its noises before he learned it by affection: the pipes knocking awake before dawn, the landlord's measured steps on the stairs, the microwave door closing in the kitchen with the dull little violence of shared living. In Korea, he had been late but legible. In New Jersey, he was simply late.

The other students on campus carried their futures carelessly, as if a major were a jacket they could change when the weather turned. Nathan carried his like a borrowed coat. Computer science sounded clean when he explained it to people back home. In English, under fluorescent classroom light, it became a list of things he did not know.

That evening he was cutting onions because Victor had asked him to help.

"Just a few people," Victor had said.

Nathan had assumed he meant the Korean graduate students: men with tired eyes, women with practical haircuts, someone always arguing about politics over beer bought too early and drunk too warm. He did not expect the voices at the door to be young, bright, female.

Victor crossed the kitchen before Nathan could put down the knife. His smile had changed already. It had become wider, younger, less honest.

The first woman came in laughing. The second apologized for being late. The third held a paper bag against her chest as if it contained something fragile. Then Elaine Park stepped in behind them, and the narrow hallway seemed to rearrange itself around her.

She was not beautiful in the delicate way Korean men praised when they wanted agreement. She was tall, sun-browned, composed in her own skin. She removed her shoes without making a performance of knowing how. She looked once toward the kitchen, not long enough to invite anything, but long enough for Nathan to become aware of the onion on the board, the knife in his hand, the shirt he had not thought to change.

Victor was already speaking too loudly.

Nathan looked down before anyone could catch him looking. It was the first cowardice Elaine would never know about.
