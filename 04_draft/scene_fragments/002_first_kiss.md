## Draft Fragment

The second gathering was easier because Nathan knew enough to be uneasy.

Victor called it a party, but it was not a party in the American sense. It was six people in a shared house near campus, beer sweating on the table, a foil tray of chicken wings going soft under its own steam, and the men moving around the kitchen as if usefulness could make them less obvious. Victor had bought too much beer. Theo Kwon, who studied economics and wore his uncertainty like a pressed shirt, kept asking where the napkins were.

The women arrived already laughing.

Nathan had begun to understand that northern New Jersey contained more Korea than he had expected: markets with handwritten signs, hair salons with women smoking by the back door, restaurants where the stews smelled like someone's mother had been angry at dawn. Across the river, Queens had even more. Still, the women who came into Victor's house did not belong to Korea in the way Nathan did. They moved through Korean things lightly. They picked them up, laughed over them, set them down again, and returned to English when Korean became too small for what they meant.

That night they talked about music. Seo Taiji made them curious. H.O.T. made them laugh. Fin.K.L. made one of the women clap her hands and try to remember a dance step she had seen somewhere, maybe on a videotape, maybe at a cousin's house. Nathan watched them take the culture he had come from and turn it in their hands like an object bought from a market stall. He should have been offended. Instead, he was fascinated.

Elaine sat on the arm of the sofa with a beer bottle between her fingers, not performing shyness for anyone.

When Jane said, "Let's go sing," Victor agreed too quickly.

The karaoke place near campus had the familiar private rooms, the sticky songbook pages, the remote control no one ever knew how to use on the first try. Nathan expected the women to sit, choose songs, laugh behind their hands. That was what he knew. That was how a room arranged itself around women in his memory: politely, indirectly, with everyone pretending not to want too much attention.

These women did not sit.

They stood before the first song had reached its chorus. They shouted over the machine. They danced in the narrow space between the table and the wall, knocking their knees against the vinyl bench, not caring. They sang American songs Nathan did not know and Korean songs they half-knew, breaking into English whenever the lyrics betrayed them.

Then Elaine climbed onto the table.

For one suspended second Nathan thought someone would tell her to come down. No one did. She lifted one hand toward the low ceiling and moved her body in a slow wave, amused by herself, unashamed of being watched. The room changed around her. Victor laughed too loudly. Theo looked at the songbook as if it might protect him. Nathan forgot the beer in his hand.

Elaine was not asking to be understood. That was what unsettled him. She was not translating herself for the Korean men in the room. She was simply there, sun-browned under the cheap lights, loose-haired, laughing, impossible to file away as modest or wild or good or dangerous. She was all of those things only because Nathan had too few categories.

Hours later, Victor pulled to the curb outside her dormitory.

It was nearly two in the morning. The road was empty enough for the car's engine to sound impolite. Elaine opened the back door and stepped out into the cold, and Nathan heard himself say he would walk her in.

Victor shrugged. "Hurry up."

Inside, the dormitory lobby was bright in the merciless way of institutional buildings after midnight. The floor shone. A bulletin board announced meetings no one would attend. Somewhere beyond the doors, the building breathed in its sleep.

Nathan and Elaine stopped where they should have said goodnight.

She looked at him first, or he decided later that she had. Her eyes were not romantic. They were warm from beer, direct, a little amused, as if she had found him standing at the edge of something and was waiting to see whether he would step forward. He could smell chicken, smoke from someone's coat, the sweet-sour trace of beer between them.

He kissed her because not kissing her had become the stranger choice.

At first it was only the pressure of her mouth, soft and brief enough to deny. Then she did not move away. His hand found her waist. Her fingers closed around the front of his shirt. The kiss deepened with an awkward hunger that made him feel younger than he was and older than he wanted to be. They moved without deciding to the sofa in the first-floor lounge, where the vinyl stuck faintly to the backs of his hands and the fluorescent lights made privacy impossible.

No one came through. Or if they did, Nathan never remembered them.

He remembered her breath. He remembered the small sound she made when he pulled back and returned. He remembered thinking, absurdly, that America had too many public places where a person could still disappear.

When they finally stood, the lobby clock had become an accusation.

Outside, Victor's car was still at the curb.

Nathan saw the shape of him through the windshield, head tipped back, one hand on the steering wheel. For a moment Nathan felt the whole night divide itself in two: Elaine behind him, warm and real and already receding; Victor ahead of him, waiting in the cold because Nathan had wanted something and taken it.

He walked toward the car slowly, carrying the taste of Elaine's mouth like evidence.
