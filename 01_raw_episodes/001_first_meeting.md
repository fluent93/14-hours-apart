## 1. Factual Memory

Nathan Kim is near graduation from a prestigious Korean university in the late 1990s. He studied education largely because of family influence, especially his father's advice, but he feels little attachment to the field. After military service, he returns to school without a clear professional direction, completing credits because graduation requires it rather than because he has found a future.

His classmates divide themselves into predictable tracks: corporate employment, graduate school, or national examinations. Nathan dislikes the idea of ordinary employment, does not want graduate study in a subject that bores him, and has no interest in examinations. During a phone call with an uncle living in the United States, he begins to consider computers and the internet as a serious future.

The moment belongs to the late-1990s transition into the digital age: Korean students still use PC communication services, personal mobile phones are becoming common, the internet feels slow and unfinished, Yahoo dominates the online world, Amazon is being mentioned in university lectures, and Google has not yet become the obvious center of search.

Nathan decides, in his abrupt way, to change fields entirely. Rather than transfer within Korea, he chooses to study computer science in the United States. Friends and senior students tell him it is reckless to abandon a humanities/social-science background and enter a technical field as an older undergraduate. Nathan goes anyway.

He enrolls as an undergraduate at a fictional New Jersey state university because he lacks the technical background for graduate school. He is older than most students, culturally out of place, and not fluent enough in English to move comfortably through campus life. He eats alone, studies alone, goes to the gym alone, and slowly comes to know a few older Korean graduate students.

Nathan lives in a shared house near campus with several international housemates. The landlord also lives there and works in finance. Nathan has a large room on the second floor. One day, an older Korean doctoral student moves into the room across from him.

The older student, working name Victor Han, is a Korean graduate student in communications or media studies and is involved in the Korean graduate student community. He is older, sociable, politically opinionated, and interested in women in a way Nathan finds increasingly uncomfortable. Because they are both Korean, both older than typical undergraduates, and living across the hall from each other, Nathan and the older student become close quickly.

They often drink at home because late-night drinking culture does not translate easily to suburban American college life. Their conversations move from Korean politics and power to loneliness, women, and the strange isolation of studying abroad.

The older student teaches or assists with a class connected to Korea, Korean language, or Korean culture. Many students are Korean American or 1.5-generation Korean immigrants, not international students from Korea. The older student is especially interested in the attractive women in this class, and Nathan senses that his attention is not purely academic.

The older student invites several female students to the shared house for dinner and asks Nathan to help prepare food and drinks. Nathan assumes graduate-school colleagues are coming and does not fully understand the older student's social intention. Around seven in the evening, four young women arrive.

Among them is the woman who will become Elaine Park in the novel. Nathan notices her immediately: tall, poised, sun-browned, physically self-possessed, and different from the campus loneliness he has grown used to. Their first meeting happens not in a dramatic romantic setting, but in a slightly awkward house dinner arranged by someone else's motives.

## 2. Sensory Details

- Late-1990s Korea: PCS phones, PC communication services, slow internet, the feeling that the world is about to change but has not yet changed.
- Nathan's Korean campus life: classmates preparing for companies, graduate school, or exams.
- American campus: younger undergraduates, English moving too quickly, Nathan's age and cultural distance making him visible and invisible at once.
- House share near campus: second-floor rooms, several international tenants, landlord in the same house, hallway proximity.
- Home drinking: beer or whiskey from a liquor store, simple Korean-style food such as sausage and vegetables, conversation stretching late because there is nowhere else to go.
- Dinner party: food preparation, alcohol bought in advance, a group of four women arriving in the evening, Nathan's surprise when he realizes this is not the gathering he expected.
- First sight of Elaine: height, tan skin, composure, sexual confidence or vitality without needing to be overstated.

## 3. Emotional Truth

Nathan feels displaced before he ever meets Elaine. He is older than the students around him, less fluent than he wants to be, and unable to explain his life without sounding defensive. The move to America is an act of courage, but it also leaves him exposed.

He is not yet a man with a finished identity. He is a man trying to become someone else before he knows what that person will cost him.

The older Korean roommate gives him familiarity, language, and companionship, but also introduces discomfort: male loneliness turning into entitlement, desire covered by jokes, and an immigrant social world where boundaries are blurred by isolation.

Elaine's entrance matters because Nathan sees someone who does not fit his small survival routine. She is not only attractive. She belongs to a version of America he cannot yet reach: fluent, embodied, socially easy, and not waiting for permission.

What is not said: Nathan is lonely. He is embarrassed by his loneliness. He wants to be seen without needing to explain why he is behind.

## 4. Conflict Potential

- Attraction: Nathan's immediate awareness of Elaine can become the first spark.
- Misreading: Nathan may mistake Elaine's ease for indifference or confidence without cost.
- Cultural tension: Nathan is Korean-born and newly arrived as an older undergraduate; Elaine can be written as Korean-born but American-formed after immigration.
- Boundary discomfort: the older student's interest in younger women creates a morally uneasy social setup.
- Class and age tension: Nathan is not a typical undergraduate, and Elaine may not initially know what to make of him.
- Shame: Nathan does not want to appear lost, poor in English, or socially dependent on older Korean graduate students.

## 5. Fictionalization Notes

- Replace all real or potentially real names with fictional names. Use Nathan Kim and Elaine Park for the central characters.
- The university must remain a fictional New Jersey state university.
- Do not use the exact Korean university, exact department, or exact title of the older student's academic role.
- The finance employer of the landlord is identifying and should be generalized or omitted.
- The older Korean roommate should be a composite character. Working name suggestion: Victor Han.
- Avoid transcript-like reuse of real dialogue. Preserve the social dynamic rather than exact phrasing.
- The older student's inappropriate interest in students is narratively useful but privacy-sensitive. Fictionalize his role so he is not traceable to a real instructor, student leader, or department.
- Preserve the late-1990s digital atmosphere, but avoid overloading the scene with historical explanation.

## 7. English Fiction Direction

This should not be translated as exposition. The novel version should open inside Nathan's American loneliness, then let his earlier Korean decision appear through controlled memory.

Preferred prose mode:

- Close third person, Nathan's POV.
- Literary-commercial romance tone.
- Concrete images over historical explanation.
- Let late-1990s references appear through objects and habits, not lectures.
- Make Elaine's entrance quiet but charged.

Possible scene pressure:

Nathan believes he is helping with an ordinary Korean graduate-student dinner. When the guests arrive, he realizes the evening has another purpose. Before he can decide how he feels about that, Elaine steps into the house, and his private loneliness changes shape.
