# Episode 02 - First Kiss

Source: Google Drive document `Episode 02 - 첫 키스`

## 1. Factual Memory

Elaine Park's first impression on Nathan Kim is not overwhelming, but it is attractive enough to remain with him. The first house gathering, which Nathan joined almost by accident, becomes unexpectedly enjoyable because he is lonely and bored in America. After that, the small dinners Victor Han organizes begin to feel like a new form of social life.

Northern New Jersey and New York both have large Korean communities. Fort Lee and Flushing offer Korean restaurants, markets, salons, bars, and even Korean-style bathhouse culture. The fictional university is in northern New Jersey, close enough to New York that the distance feels, to Nathan, like Seoul to Seongnam. There is also a Korean karaoke place near campus.

At a later gathering, several of the same women return. This time the group includes three women from the earlier dinner, Nathan, Victor, and another Korean male student studying economics. Working fictional name: Theo Kwon. The gathering is closer to a casual dinner than an American-style house party. The men prepare beer and popular chicken wings from a crowded local restaurant near campus.

The evening becomes loud and easy. People drink beer, eat chicken wings, and talk about Korean pop music and films. Korean pop culture is changing: Seo Taiji has already shifted the culture, and groups like H.O.T. and Fin.K.L. are becoming icons for young Koreans. But the Korean American women respond differently from Korean-born students. To them, Korean pop culture is interesting, even amusing, but not necessarily identity-defining.

Nathan notices how American these women are in their emotional habits. They are direct, expressive, and less invested in pretending modesty or restraint. When they are happy and tipsy, they speak loudly, sing pop songs the older Korean men barely know, try Korean until it becomes inconvenient, then slip back into English with relief.

Jane suggests karaoke. The group goes to the Korean karaoke place near campus.

Nathan, Victor, and Theo are shocked by what happens there. This is not how women behaved in Korean karaoke rooms as Nathan remembers them. The women do not sit politely. They stand, jump, dance, sing, and take over the room. Elaine, whom Nathan had thought might be relatively reserved, climbs onto the table and dances with a fluid, confident wave of her body.

Nathan never forgets that image.

Later, when everyone separates, Nathan and Elaine ride back in Victor's car. Victor stops near Elaine's dormitory late at night. Because it is around 2 a.m., Nathan offers to walk Elaine from the curb to her dorm building while Victor waits in the car.

Inside the dorm entrance, at the point where they should say goodnight, Nathan and Elaine look at each other with the warm, sticky, beer-scented intensity of a night that has gone too far to remain ordinary. They kiss naturally. It does not remain a brief goodnight kiss. The kiss deepens, and they spend more than two hours kissing on a common sofa in the dorm's first-floor lounge.

This is around 2000, before everyone carries a mobile phone. Nathan had used a PCS phone in Korea and does not have a convenient American cell phone. Elaine does not have one either. Communication often still depends on landlines, answering machines, and whoever in the group has a phone. Victor, as the student leader and driver, is the one with a mobile phone.

Because Nathan stays with Elaine, Victor waits and waits outside, effectively stood up by Nathan.

## 2. Sensory Details

- Northern New Jersey and New York Korean enclaves: Korean markets, salons, bars, restaurants, bathhouse culture, late-night familiarity inside suburban America.
- Campus-adjacent Korean karaoke: private room, microphones, table, beer, pop songs, bodies moving in a way Nathan does not expect.
- House dinner: beer, chicken wings, the smell of fried food, men doing the preparation, women arriving into a setting made for them.
- Korean pop references: Seo Taiji, H.O.T., Fin.K.L., and the gap between Korean-born nostalgia and Korean American curiosity.
- Language switching: women trying Korean, then giving up and moving into English among themselves.
- Elaine dancing on the table: wave-like movement, confidence, Nathan's shock and fascination.
- Dorm entrance at 2 a.m.: empty public space, fluorescent light, beer on breath, sofa in the first-floor lounge, Victor's car waiting outside.
- Time-period texture: few cell phones, answering machines, dependence on drivers and shared plans.

## 3. Emotional Truth

Nathan is lonely enough that the social world around Victor becomes attractive even when he senses something uncomfortable in it. He wants fun, company, and access to a kind of American college life he has been watching from the outside.

Elaine becomes more interesting because she surprises him. She is not simply pretty. She does not behave according to the categories Nathan brought from Korea. Her confidence in the karaoke room is disorienting because it is public, bodily, and unashamed.

Nathan's attraction shifts from visual interest to physical longing. The kiss happens because the night has already dissolved several boundaries: language, alcohol, Korean restraint, American directness, guest and host, student and outsider.

What is not said: Nathan does not know whether Elaine is choosing him specifically or choosing the mood of the night. Elaine may not know yet either. Victor's presence outside the dorm adds guilt and comic awkwardness, but also a subtle moral pressure: Nathan's desire has consequences for someone else, even in this small way.

## 4. Conflict Potential

- Attraction becomes physical intimacy.
- Nathan experiences Elaine as freer than he is, but may also misread freedom as availability.
- The kiss creates a private bond before either character has language for what it means.
- Victor's waiting car creates immediate guilt and social complication.
- The language/culture divide is visible through music, drinking, karaoke behavior, and code-switching.
- The episode can later contrast with present-day restraint: older Nathan and Elaine may remember the same kiss differently.

## 5. Fictionalization Notes

- Keep Nathan Kim, Elaine Park, and Victor Han as fictional working names.
- Make Theo Kwon a composite for the economics student; do not preserve a real name.
- Jane may remain a minor fictional/composite name unless later privacy concerns suggest changing it.
- Fort Lee and Flushing are real public places, but use them sparingly. If privacy risk rises, generalize to northern New Jersey and Queens Korean enclaves.
- Do not identify the chicken wing restaurant, karaoke business, dormitory, university, or exact route.
- Do not reproduce real private dialogue as transcript.
- Keep the first kiss sensual but restrained; avoid memoir-like explicitness.

## 7. English Fiction Direction

This scene should move more like fiction than memory: start with the second gathering already in motion, let the Korean enclave and time-period texture appear through concrete details, then build toward Elaine dancing and the late-night kiss.

Preferred prose mode:

- Close third person, Nathan's POV.
- Sensual but not graphic.
- Let Elaine remain partially unknowable; Nathan should be aware of his own misreadings.
- Use Victor's waiting car as the ending pressure.
- Make the early-2000s lack of phones matter narratively, not nostalgically.
